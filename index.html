Trang Huynh

I'm currently a Ph.D. student at Department of Physics and Astronomy at Louisiana State University (LSU). I graduated with a B.A. in Physics with a concentration in Astronomy from LSU in 2023.

Currently, I am working with Prof. Manos Chatzopolous on computational and theoretical aspects of massive star evolution, supernovae, and peculiar transient phenomena. I explore the effects of rotation, initial mass, and metallicity on Pulsational Pair Instability Supernovae, using tools like MESA and radiation transport code to model stellar dynamics, energetics, and spectral properties.

Complementing my research goals, I'm also passionate about supporting young female physicists and creating a welcoming space within my astronomy department. I co-founded LSU Chapter of Women in Physics Club, with the goal of supporting and retaining women and gender minority students in their physics education. I also host Astrophysics Journal Club at LSU and involve in various organizations such as P&A GSO and APS Chapter at LSU. In my spare time, I like cooking, running, and gardening.

Rotational Dynamics on Pulsational Pair Instability Supernovae

Pulsational pair-instability supernovae (PPISNe) are transient events occurring in progenitor stars with helium cores of \~32-65 solar masses, where rapid electron-positron pair production induces pressure loss, collapse, and pulsations driving episodic mass loss. The number, strength, and duration of these pulses can lead to shell collisions that produce shock-powered transients, potentially explaining some of the most luminous events, such as superluminous supernovae, and other rare transients. I computed 1D evolutionary models of massive, rotating PPISN progenitor stars. Our analysis reveals strong correlations between PPISN progenitor mass leads to greater mass ejection and energy release, while rapid rotation appears to reduce mass ejection and kinetic energy of the shells. Subsequent pulses lead to H-poor, C- and O-enriched ejected shells, indicating the effect of rotationally-induced mixing PPISN-driven episodic mass-loss with implications for their transients. We also found that shock-heated H-poor PPISN shell collisions from rapidly rotating progenitors can lead to moderately-luminous H-poor transients that share some similarities with observed SLSN-I events.
